Walt Disney Concert Hall

Basic Facts

Name: Walt Disney Concert Hall

Location: 111 South Grand Avenue, Los Angeles, CA

Architect: Frank Gehry

Constructed: 1987 ➡️ October 2003 [1]

Purpose

  • The WDCH is home to the Los Angeles Philharmonic and serves as a venue for many other music groups.
  • Lillian Disney donated to the Music Center, which has historically been close to the Disney family, to have this concert hall built. She deemed the hall to be a "permanent tribute to [her] late husband, Walt Disney" [2].
  • The building also serves as a "living room for the city" [2]. The curved design of the lobby for the building encourages conversation, movement, and has a welcoming mood to it. It stands out from its relatively static urban environment and provides a friendly atmosphere.
  • While the concert hall is primarily for people who want to attend a concert, it is also a common place for residents to just "come together and feel comfortable doing so" [2].

Exterior

Walt Disney Concert Hall at Night

Materials: stainless steel, glass, concrete [4]



caption:

The thousands of unique stainless steel panels add a surprising deal of texture to the "sails" of the building. The texture on each sail varies, with some having very smooth and large panels and others having many small panels that give off a more scaly texture.

Fun fact: Gehry's originally intended for the building to be made of stone rather than its iconic stainless steel.

Isometric View of the Walt Disney Concert Hall [5]


caption:

The concert hall also has an interesting sense of balance to it. Looking at it from afar, the building feels quite cohesive as the sails on one side of the building are contrasted by those on the other side. There is a harmonious mix between convex and concave sails.

The Walt Disney Concert Hall stands out in its urban surroundings largely because of its shapes. Its unique convex and concave sails appear flexible and fluid, standing in stark contrast to that of the city's rigid walls and streets.

Google Earth View

The Walt Disney Concert Hall is in an urban setting. The surrounding buildings all follow the same structure: they have white walls and lots of windows. The hall stands out in that it is made of steel and has an absence of windows. The steel sails appear sort of symbolically as the sails bounce off the rest of the city's atmosphere. The hall creates its own bubble of influence and vibes.

Interior

Walt Disney Concert Hall Lobby [6]


caption:

The curved shape of the outside of the concert hall is maintained from the exterior to the interior of the building. There is, however, a soft transition in color and material: the outside is stainless steel and the inside has wood panels. The curved walls of the building make the lobby appear more open and fluid. This is good for the function of a lobby where people can socialize and follow the flow of the building.

Scholarly Articles

Concert Halls by Nagata Acoustics

summary: The lobby being distributed over multiple floors is intended to add intimacy to the musical experience as people checking in feel like they are attending a smaller concert than they really are. The concert hall itself took many of the acoustic factors into account with its internal curved design. The concavity of the place resulted in problems that needed to be addressed like focusing acoustics with panels and rearranging seating thirty times. [3]

citation:
Toyota, Y., Komoda, M., Beckmann, D., Quiquerez, M., Bergal, E. (2020). Walt Disney Concert Hall. In: Concert Halls by Nagata Acoustics . Springer, Cham. https://doi.org/10.1007/ 978-3-030-42450-3_5

Microclimatic Impact: Glare Around the Walt Disney Concert Hall

summary: Building materials and curvature are factors that need to be properly addressed with modern architecture as it can negatively impact its miroclimate -- its immediate surrounding area. When walking around certain areas of the building, it is possible to feel more intense heat and light. Visual discomfort and intense solar glare are risks that come with using a reflective material like steel. Aesthetic is one thing to handle, safety is another. [7]

My Drawing

I tried to capture the concert hall's fluid and playful nature. Every aspect from the railings to the sails has a curve and warped look to it. I think this unique shape to the building is critical to capture because it is reminiscent of Walt Disney's legacy: his imagination fueled a great deal of our childhood memories.

The top of the Walt Disney Concert Hall reminds me of Mickey Mouse's ears, and I wanted to accentuate that feature in my drawing. Also, I made sure that the sails had different patterns to them because it would not feel right to make a building dedicated to Disney's legacy so uniform and static.

Other

Video Feature [8]

The outside of the concert hall can also be used as a way to display art. Using projectors and machine learning, it is possible to display an image or video across the sails of the hall.

The hall's sails are already artistic and unique; however, the addition of the ability to display color and life onto them adds that much more grandeur and awe to the building.

Interview with Frank Gehry [9]

Although the exterior gets the most attention, Gehry's design and ideas revolve around the inside. For example, in the music hall, $5 million dollars was spent on wood decoration because of its psychological effect: there is a positive psychological impact with wood as people associate orchestral music with it. Gehry also alludes to the principle of "form follows function" by talking about the issue of connecting the toilets to the stairs. Transitioning to the exterior, Gehry says that he originally wanted stone because it would pick up ambient light and have soft lighting easily, but switched to metal after people lauded the usage of titanium at Bilbao.

Blog: Rite of Spring: Frank Gehry and the Walt Disney Concert Hall of the LA Philharmonic

  • In the competition for the concert hall commission, Gehry was voted unanimously despite being a sort of underdog. His design took guidelines and the environment around where the hall would be built into account. His design was genius for its "treatment of public circulation spaces" [10].
  • Rather than focus on theory, Gehry relied on intuition and pragmatism. A lot of his thought process was enacted through "chunky abstract wooden blocks and cardboard slabs" [10].
  • The Walt Disney Concert Hall is intended to be a metaphorical flower. In terms of the exterior architecture, it mimics a "giant Easter lily with walls like spreading petals" [10]. It is meant to vividly represent LA as both the building and the city blossom in their surroundings. Gehry thinks its representation of LA will rival that of the Sydney Opera House's representation of Australia.

Blog: Review of Disney Hall: Reflection of the city around it

The author argues that the most moving part of the concert hall is that it recognizes that "ideal beauty rarely exists in an imperfect world" [11]. They also argue that it expresses modern cultural values and is a work of social commentary. The piece goes into depth about aspects of the building that articulate the subtle depths of it, but I do not really see it. While I admire the architecture and attention to detail. After watching the interview with Frank Gehry and reading this post, I respect the building a lot more as there was a lot of work that went into even small psychological aspects. However, the post appears a bit too pretentious about the social commentary aspect. Its claims remain too unsubstantiated to influence me.
